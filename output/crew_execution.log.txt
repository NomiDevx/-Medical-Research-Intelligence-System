## **KEY LITERATURE FINDINGS**

### **1. Renal Protection with GLP-1 Receptor Agonists**
The FLOW trial (Perkovic et al., 2024) represents a landmark advancement in renal protection for T2D patients with CKD [1]. This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolled 3,533 participants with T2D and CKD (eGFR 50-75 mL/min/1.73 m² with UACR >300 mg/g, or eGFR 25-50 mL/min/1.73 m² with UACR >100 mg/g). Once-weekly subcutaneous semaglutide (up to 2.0 mg) reduced the risk of the primary composite outcome (kidney disease progression or death from cardiovascular causes) by 24% compared to placebo (hazard ratio 0.76; 95% CI 0.66-0.88; P=0.0003). The trial was stopped early at a prespecified interim analysis due to clear efficacy.

### **2. Cardiovascular Outcomes with Dual Incretin Agonists**
The cardiovascular outcomes trial comparing tirzepatide to dulaglutide (Nicholls et al., 2025) provides critical safety data for this novel dual agonist [2]. This active-comparator-controlled, double-blind, noninferiority trial enrolled patients with T2D and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. The primary endpoint was a composite of death from cardiovascular causes, myocardial infarction, or stroke. Tirzepatide demonstrated noninferiority to dulaglutide with respect to cardiovascular outcomes, establishing its safety profile in high-risk populations.

### **3. Combination Renal Protective Therapy**
Agarwal et al. (2025) investigated the synergistic effects of combining finerenone (a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist) with empagliflozin (an SGLT2 inhibitor) in patients with CKD and T2D [3]. The randomized trial showed that combination therapy reduced urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio by 29% more than finerenone alone (least-squares mean ratio 0.71; 95% CI 0.61-0.82; P<0.001) at 180 days. This supports the concept of combined pathway inhibition for enhanced renal protection.

### **4. Oral GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Cardiovascular Efficacy**
The SOUL trial (McGuire et al., 2025) evaluated cardiovascular outcomes with oral semaglutide in high-risk T2D patients [4]. This double-blind, placebo-controlled, event-driven superiority trial enrolled participants ≥50 years with T2D (HbA1c 6.5-10.0%) and established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, CKD, or both. Once-daily oral semaglutide (maximum dose 14 mg) significantly reduced the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events compared to placebo, expanding treatment options to include oral administration of GLP-1 RAs.

### **5. Clinical Practice Guidelines**
The American College of Physicians guideline (Qaseem et al., 2024) provides evidence-based recommendations for newer pharmacologic treatments in T2D [5]. Based on systematic review of GLP-1 agonists, GIP/GLP-1 dual agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, DPP-4 inhibitors, and long-acting insulins, the guideline prioritizes agents with demonstrated cardiovascular and renal benefits, emphasizing personalized treatment selection based on individual patient characteristics and comorbidities.

### **6. Comparative Effectiveness of GLP-1 Receptor Agonists**
Yao et al. (2024) conducted a comprehensive network meta-analysis of 76 randomized controlled trials involving 39,246 participants and 15 GLP-1 RA drugs [6]. The analysis found that tirzepatide and high-dose semaglutide were the most effective agents for reducing HbA1c and body weight. The study provides a detailed ranking of GLP-1 RAs based on efficacy outcomes, supporting personalized treatment decisions.

### **7. Functional Benefits in Peripheral Artery Disease**
The STRIDE trial (Bonaca et al., 2025) investigated the effects of semaglutide on walking capacity in patients with T2D and symptomatic peripheral artery disease [7]. This phase 3b, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial demonstrated that semaglutide significantly improved walking distance and patient-reported outcomes, suggesting potential benefits beyond traditional metabolic parameters.

### **8. Novel Oral Small-Molecule GLP-1 Receptor Agonist**
Rosenstock et al. (2025) evaluated orforglipron, an oral small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist, in treatment-naïve patients with early T2D [8]. The phase 3 trial showed dose-dependent efficacy for glycemic control and weight reduction, offering a convenient oral alternative to injectable GLP-1 RAs.
